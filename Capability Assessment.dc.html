What This Build Can Do — and What It Can't

The goal: a standalone, multi-tenant Billing Portal where AI agents run a billing company's revenue cycle and humans supervise by exception — governed and onboarded through Operations Admin. This is an honest audit of every screen against that goal: what a viewer can experience today, what is designed but simulated, and what does not exist yet.

1 · Where the loop stands

The supervision loop for primary claims is closed end to end: prevent (Coverage) → generate & submit (Agent Inbox pipeline) → track (Claims Register / Claim Detail) → work denials (AR Worklist) → supervise (Agent Inbox decisions + QA) → learn (Rulebook) → measure (Analytics) → govern (Settings + Ops Portal Config). What happens after the primary payer pays — posting cash, secondary claims, patient balances — is designed on paper (Phases 7–8) but has no screens.

6 · Not real — prototype limits behind the screens

Everything above is a design prototype. These limits don't show in a demo but matter for anyone mistaking it for a working system.

  • The agents are scripted, not live. Every narrative, evidence panel, and proposed fix is authored. No model reads real notes, calls real payers, or generates real claims.
  • All data is synthetic. One shared deterministic claims store (seeded from the real AR report you gave me) drives every screen. No clearinghouse, no X12 (837/835/270/271) wiring, no EMR connection.
  • State lives in the browser. Approvals, QA reviews, escalations, and settings persist in localStorage — per browser, per person. There is no backend, no shared state between viewers.
  • Auth is simulated. The role switcher (rep ↔ manager) and practice switcher stand in for login and tenancy. The boundary is designed, not enforced.
  • One tenant is fleshed out. Bell Medics with two practices (Ibekie Foundation, Westbrook). Multi-company works in Ops screens but only one billing-portal tenant has depth.
  • Edges are curated. Global search is decorative; a handful of buttons are acknowledged stubs (tracked in the QA Checklist); cross-screen links follow the demo paths, not every permutation.

7 · Even when built for real — three data risks

From the Data Feasibility doc: most screens reach ~80–100% data coverage through standard clearinghouse transactions, but three things no API fully provides — prior authorization (X12 278 adoption is spotty), COB primacy (confirming payer order is a phone/portal workflow), and legacy AR backfill (claims billed on a prior PM system need negotiated data access). The design already routes all three through the agent-worked channel, but they are the slowest, most manual part of any real deployment.

Bottom line: you can demo a complete, governed, agent-run primary-claim revenue cycle — from pre-visit prevention to denial resolution to autonomy tiers earned through QA evidence — with a credible ops story around it. You cannot yet show money being posted, a balance surviving past the primary payer, or the practice seeing any of it. Those three (Phase 6c, 7, 8) are the difference between "supervision cockpit" and "a billing company's whole system."
